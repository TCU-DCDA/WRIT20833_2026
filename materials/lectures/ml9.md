You have counted the words, scored the feeling, and modeled the themes of 123 comments about a contested
law. You can say, with evidence, what that crowd was arguing about and how it felt. Here is the question
this whole course has been walking toward: **so what — and who is it for?** An analysis that never leaves
your laptop changes exactly nothing. Today is about the last move, the one that turns a finding into an
argument a human can act on.

## The notebook is not the destination

It is easy to mistake the analysis for the finish line. You ran the cells, the chart rendered, the topics
came back — done. But a result sitting in a private notebook is knowledge **locked away**: trapped on your
machine, written in a dialect (pandas, VADER, LDA) that the people your analysis is *about* cannot read.
The commenters whose words you counted will never see what you found. The point of hearing the human at
scale was never to file the hearing away. It was to **say something back.**

## What a public argument is

A public argument takes a scholarly or cultural insight and makes it **accessible, engaging, and
actionable** for people beyond this seminar. Not dumbed down — translated. The difference is plain
arithmetic: a result you keep reaches the handful of people already in the room; a result you frame for an
audience can reach everyone who needs it.

> A finding in a journal nobody outside the field reads has eight readers. The same finding, argued for a
> public, can have eight thousand. The analysis is identical. What changed is that you decided to be
> *understood.*

In this course your public artifact isn't a website — that delivery belongs to a different class. It's your
**capstone: a data-driven-opinion essay and a short presentation.** Same move, humbler medium.

## Your skills are already a public intervention

This isn't theory. Consider *Mapping Police Violence* — a project built on exactly the skills you have been
learning: collect public records, analyze them for pattern over time and geography, visualize the result,
and put a clear public face on it. The technical work is ordinary — scraping, a DataFrame, a chart. What
makes it **matter** is that it made an invisible pattern visible to journalists, families, and
policymakers, and got cited in debates that move real lives.

That is the whole equation: *the same analysis skills you practiced this month + a public face = a cultural
intervention.* You are closer to that than you think.

## You are joining an old tradition

Going public is not a new internet trick. It runs back through the **public intellectuals** who wrote for
newspapers instead of only for each other, the **community archives** that preserved neighborhood memory
the official record skipped, and the **oral-history projects** that carried marginalized voices into the
permanent record. Each one made the same bet you are about to make: that expert knowledge has a duty to
**reach the people it concerns,** not just circulate among experts. Accessibility isn't a courtesy here.
It's an ethic.

## How you present *is* the argument

Here is the rhetorical catch, and it carries straight over from the Connotations lecture. The choices you
make in presenting an analysis are not neutral packaging — they **argue.** What you put first and what you
bury in an appendix; which number you make the headline; the chart you reach for and the one you don't:
every one is a claim about what matters, aimed at a reader. Structure is rhetoric. Code is rhetoric.

So the standard from day one holds in public, too — **show your seams.** An honest public argument says
what it counted *and what it left out,* names the cutoff it chose and why, and admits where it might be
wrong. A polished take that hides its choices isn't more persuasive; it's just less honest. Being willing
to be wrong out loud is not a weakness in a public argument. It's the thing that makes it trustworthy.

## What you do with what you found

Knowledge becomes wisdom only in what you **do** with it. The reason to hear a crowd at scale is, in the
end, a moral one: to make someone who didn't know to care begin to care — and you can't move anyone with a
finding they never see. That is the work this last move is for.

One caution carried from the whole term: going public is not flattening. Your job is not to resolve the
quarrel into a tidy verdict the way a single topic-list pretends a messy debate was clean. The honest public
argument **preserves the disagreement** even as it takes a position — it gives voice to the crowd without
pretending the crowd agreed.

> The reflection that becomes your capstone: *what did your analysis find that someone outside this room needs to see — who are they, and what should they do once they have seen it?*

You already have the insight. The last skill is the courage and the craft to hand it to the people it's for.
