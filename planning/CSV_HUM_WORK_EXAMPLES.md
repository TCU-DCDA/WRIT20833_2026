# TCU Core vetting — student-work examples (CSV + HUM)

**Purpose.** WRIT 20833 carries two TCU Core Curriculum designations — **Citizenship & Social Values
(CSV)** and **Humanities (HUM)**. Core re-vetting asks for **2–3 concrete examples of student work** that
achieve each outcome, plus how the work is assessed. This doc drafts those examples (full set of three
each — **trim to the submission form's field limits**). Source context: `planning/CONCEPTUAL_FRAMEWORK_2026.md`
§7; outcome wording verified against `reference/TCU-Core-Curriculum-outcomes-1.pdf`.

**The running case** the examples draw on: a contested 2025 public-policy debate — a Texas law placing the
Ten Commandments in public-school classrooms — studied through a corpus of **123 real public comments**
alongside the **U.S. Constitution** the commenters keep invoking. Methods build across the term (term
frequency → sentiment → topic modeling → integration) into a **capstone** (data-driven-opinion notebook +
essay). Evaluation is **ungrading**: a 3-point engagement scale (3 exceeds / 2 meets / 1 not-yet) on
honest engagement + reflection, *not* correctness; the reflective write-ups and `#comments` are where the
outcome evidence shows.

---

## CSV — Citizenship & Social Values
**Student Learning Outcome:** *Examine the knowledge, skills, values, or motivation needed to participate
or lead within diverse communities.* *(Competency context: Responsible Citizenship — concepts of social
responsibility in diverse communities.)*

### Example 1 — Capstone data-driven-opinion essay *(central artifact / Final Evaluative Exercise)*
- **What the student does.** Choosing a contested public debate (the provided Ten Commandments corpus or
  their own collected dataset), the student forms and argues an **evidence-based opinion** grounded in
  their own computational analysis, and must **give voice to the community in disagreement** — including
  the side they personally oppose — while naming where their methods risk flattening those voices.
- **How it meets the outcome.** It is, directly, an exercise in *examining the knowledge, skills, and
  values needed to participate in a diverse community*: the student weighs the competing values at stake
  (e.g., religious expression vs. church–state separation), represents the rights and positions of groups
  who disagree, and practices the literacy — reasoning from evidence about a public-policy choice — that
  responsible civic participation requires.
- **How it's assessed.** 3-point engagement scale + the essay's argument and its honest "what my method
  got wrong" reckoning; presented to peers on the final day.

### Example 2 — Four threaded discussions (D1–D4)
- **What the student does.** Each week the student posts to and replies substantively in a discussion on a
  contested question — *Is code neutral? Whose data, and is it ethical to use it? What does close vs.
  distant reading reveal and hide? What does computation reveal and conceal about a public?* — engaging
  classmates who hold different views.
- **How it meets the outcome.** The discussions *are* participation within a diverse community in
  disagreement: students advance positions with evidence and care, weigh peers' competing values, and
  practice the deliberative skills of civic discourse on real social questions.
- **How it's assessed.** 3-point scale on the initial post + two substantive replies (timeliness and
  genuine engagement with others' positions, not "correctness").

### Example 3 — Self-reflections (R1 "Where I'm starting" + R3 final self-evaluation)
- **What the student does.** In R1 the student articulates prior assumptions about what computers can and
  can't tell us about culture and people; in R3 they make an evidence-based case for their own growth,
  including how responsibly they *heard* (vs. flattened) the people in their data.
- **How it meets the outcome.** The reflections surface the *motivation and disposition* side of the
  outcome — examining one's own values and responsibilities as a participant who analyzes, represents, and
  speaks for members of a community.
- **How it's assessed.** 3-point scale; R3 is the final self-evaluation that anchors the course's
  ungrading.

## HUM — Humanities
**Student Learning Outcome:** *Use humanistic modes of inquiry to analyze human experiences and
expressions across space and time.*

### Example 1 — Homework analyses HW2 → HW3 → HW4 (term frequency, sentiment, topic modeling)
- **What the student does.** The student applies each method to real human expression (the public
  comments) and is required to perform the course's signature **close ↔ distant reading** move: after the
  computation, return to individual texts by hand — read the extremes, run the **human-vs-automated**
  check (where a sentiment score disagrees with a human reading), and name in `#comments` **what the tool
  flattened or missed**.
- **How it meets the outcome.** This is humanistic inquiry, not mere computation: interpretation,
  attention to context and ambiguity, and judgment about what a method reveals vs. obscures about human
  expression.
- **How it's assessed.** 3-point scale + the required interpretive write-ups and `#comments` that document
  the close/distant reasoning.

### Example 2 — Capstone notebook + essay
- **What the student does.** A sustained interpretation of a cultural corpus that **integrates** the three
  methods into a single argument, moving between **contemporary** voices (the public comments) and the
  **historical** founding document (the U.S. Constitution) the debate invokes.
- **How it meets the outcome.** It analyzes "human experiences and expressions **across space and time**"
  through humanistic modes of inquiry — close and distant reading held together — and argues an
  interpretation rather than reporting an output.
- **How it's assessed.** 3-point scale; the Final Evaluative Exercise, presented and defended.

### Example 3 — "Reading for the Seams" stylometry exercise (Day 7 + capstone track)
- **What the student does.** The student reads short passages for **voice** — distinguishing human texture
  from AI "smoothness" — by hand (close reading / an attribution map) and by measurement (function-word
  and lexical-diversity signatures), then writes about what the evidence does and doesn't support.
- **How it meets the outcome.** It is humanistic attention to **expression and authorship** — style as a
  carrier of human experience — and trains epistemic humility about interpretive claims.
- **How it's assessed.** 3-point scale on the attribution map + reflection (fits the capstone
  notebook-plus-essay format for students who take the stylometry track).
